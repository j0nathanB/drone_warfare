**Background**

Somalia has lacked functional government since 1991, when socialist president Siad Barre was overthrown by armed opposition groups and rebels. The northwestern region declared itself the independent Republic of Somaliland and achieved relative stability, while Somalia descended into civil war involving rival warlords and Islamist militants. Over two decades of violence have caused up to a million deaths.

The UN arrived in July 1992 for humanitarian relief. By December 1993, the situation deteriorated, prompting UN requests for assistance. The US deployed troops to Mogadishu, where a disastrous August 1993 battle resulted in two downed Black Hawk helicopters and eighteen American soldier deaths. Estimates suggest over 700 Somali militiamen and civilians died in related operations.

**Humanitarian Crisis**

Somalia recently experienced Africa's worst humanitarian crisis. Those requiring UN assistance increased from approximately two million in early 2011 to four million by September 2011. The Somalia Food Security and Nutrition Analysis Unit declared famine in six southern areas in 2011.

The Transitional Federal Government (TFG), established through 2002-2004 Kenya peace talks, sought to provide stability and retained international recognition, though early efforts proved unsuccessful.

**The Islamic Courts Union**

In early 2006, the militia-backed Islamic Courts Union (ICU) rose to power amid clan-based militia conflict. The organization "began to impose a strict version of sharia" and "shut down movie theaters, viewing centers for soccer matches and co-ed events such as sports."

As ICU forces advanced into Mogadishu, thousands of civilians fled the capital. By mid-2006, the ICU controlled Mogadishu and much of south and central Somalia.

**Ethiopian Intervention**

In December 2006, the TFG, supported by the Ethiopian army, launched a lengthy campaign defeating the ICU. External powers including Ethiopia, the US, and the EU feared the ICU and its militant youth wing, al-Shabaab, would establish an Islamist stronghold.

Ethiopia fought ICU militias and emerging al-Shabaab for two years, joined in January 2007 by AMISOM, a UN-created African Union peace force.

The US provided Ethiopia with "airborne reconnaissance support and 'other intelligence,'" according to the Washington Post. However, WikiLeaks cables reveal US officials urging caution, with Ambassador Donald Yamamoto warning the invasion could "prove more difficult for Ethiopia than many now imagine."

When Ethiopia withdrew in 2009, some Somalis resented the foreign intervention. A humanitarian worker told the Chicago Tribune: "They resent the Ethiopians and Americans tearing it all up, using Somalia as their battlefield against global terrorism."

**Al-Shabaab's Emergence**

The TFG regained partial control, but al-Shabaab, originally the ICU's militant wing, forged an independent identity. The group aims to dismantle the TFG and "mount sustained attacks against the transitional federal institutions and their security forces, as well as AMISOM."

In 2007, al-Shabaab's leaders claimed al-Qaeda affiliation, formally announcing this alliance on February 9, 2012. The US designated al-Shabaab a terrorist organization in February 2008.

Human Rights Watch documented widespread abuses including "beheadings, amputations, stoning and beatings, restrictions on dress and freedom of movement, enforced contributions, and forcible recruitment into the militia." The group "admits to the recruitment of children, who are represented among many recent deaths and defections."

**Kenya's Invasion**

On October 16, 2011, Kenya launched Operation Linda Nchi, citing three western kidnappings by al-Shabaab militants on Kenyan soil. However, Kenyan government spokesman Alfred Mutua indicated the kidnappings were "more a 'good launchpad'" than the sole reason, stating an operation of this magnitude cannot be planned quickly.

Mutua told the Financial Times Kenya's mission extended beyond locating kidnappers to "track down and dismantle the al-Shabaab." US officials denied coordinating with Kenya or providing direct military support, with the US formally stating on October 25 that it was "emphatically not participating in the invasion."

The conflict extended to social media, with the Kenyan army and al-Shabaab exchanging Twitter insults.

**Military Players**

**JSOC (Joint Special Operations Command)**

Formed in 1980, JSOC coordinates elite Special Forces across US military branches. Since September 11, JSOC became critical to the global war on terror, hunting operatives including Abu Musab al-Zarqawi and Osama bin Laden.

JSOC forces were active in Somalia weeks after 9/11, initially focusing on surveillance and renditions. From 2007 onward, JSOC conducted airstrikes, drone strikes, and cruise missile attacks against militants, with civilian casualties also reportedly occurring.

**AMISOM (African Union Mission in Somalia)**

Created in February 2007 with a six-month mandate, AMISOM remained in Somalia five years later. The force comprises approximately 9,500 troops primarily from Uganda and Burundi, with the UN demanding an increase to 12,000 by October 2012.

In August 2011, Human Rights Watch reported that AMISOM forces bore responsibility for "serious violations of international humanitarian law" including "indiscriminate attacks, extrajudicial killings, arbitrary arrests and detention, and unlawful forced recruitment."

**CJTF-HOA (Combined Joint Task Force – Horn of Africa)**

Based at Camp Lemmonier in Djibouti, CJTF-HOA comprises approximately 2,000 US and coalition personnel plus 1,200 private contractors. Created to accomplish Operation Enduring Freedom – Horn of Africa objectives, it conducts civil and military operations under US Africa Command (Africom).

The task force's initial aim involved detecting and destroying potential terrorist hideouts, targeting individuals, breaking logistical lines, and directly attacking groups connected to al-Qaeda. Camp Lemmonier serves as a launchpad for missiles and CIA/JSOC drones.

By 2008, US military assessments concluded the region contained "less terrorist activity than originally feared," prompting the task force to expand into civil affairs missions. By early 2011, the US military re-engaged heavily in Somalia.

**CTF-150 (Combined Task Force 150)**

Created to counter terrorism, prevent smuggling, and develop maritime security, CTF-150 has boarded vessels off Somalia's coast since 2007 seeking terrorist suspects.

Participating nations included the UK, France, Canada, Germany, Pakistan, Australia, Denmark, and the US. Operating across two million square miles covering the Gulf of Aden, Gulf of Oman, Arabian Sea, Red Sea, and northern Indian Ocean, the task force performs "Visit, Board, Search, and Seizure" (VBSS) missions on fishing boats and tankers near Somali waters to "deter individuals with links to al Qaeda and other terrorist organisations."
