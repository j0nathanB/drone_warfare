title: New rules pave way for escalating war in Somalia
date: November 19, 2017
authors: Jack Serle
content:

The Bureau of Investigative Journalism reports that U.S. drone strike operations in Somalia reached record levels in 2017, with 29 strikes conducted by November—double the annual rate under the Obama administration.

The escalation stems from policy changes introduced by the Trump administration in March 2017. The new guidelines exempted portions of Somalia from targeting restrictions that Obama had established to address civilian casualty concerns. Specifically, the Trump administration classified areas of Somalia and Yemen as "active hostilities" zones, circumventing the elaborate White House approval process previously required for strikes in non-conventional war zones.

General Thomas Waldhauser, commander of U.S. military operations in Africa, stated he would maintain Obama-era protections requiring "near-certainty that no civilians would be harmed" before authorizing strikes, despite having greater operational flexibility.

Beyond air operations, the U.S. maintains approximately 500 military personnel in Somalia, including special forces advisers supporting Somali and African Union forces. The article documents several ground operations in 2017, including an August incident that reportedly killed 10 civilians and a May operation resulting in one Navy SEAL fatality.

The increased military activity follows al Shabaab's October 2017 truck bombing in Mogadishu that killed over 350 people.
