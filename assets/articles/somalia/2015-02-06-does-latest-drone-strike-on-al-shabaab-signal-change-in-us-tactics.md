title: Does al Shabaab strike signal change in US tactics in Somalia?
date: February 6, 2015
authors: Jack Serle
content:

A January 31 US drone strike in Somalia killed al Shabaab commander Yusef Dheeq and at least four others. Pentagon press secretary Rear Admiral John Kirby confirmed the operation used "Hellfire missiles fired from UAVs" with "no US boots on the ground."

**Shift in Weaponry**

The strike represents a notable tactical evolution. Between 2007 and 2008, AC-130 gunships dominated US operations in Somalia, responsible for five of the first six confirmed attacks. Since 2011, however, drone strikes have become the primary tool—eight confirmed strikes have killed at least 23 people attributed to al Shabaab.

**Fleet Expansion**

This shift correlates with dramatic drone proliferation. The US Air Force budgeted for 37 Reaper drones in 2007 but operated 401 aircraft by 2012, according to Pentagon inspector general reports. This expansion reflected recognition of drones' effectiveness in counterterrorism campaigns.

**Precision and Civilian Concerns**

While CIA Director John Brennan credits drones with striking "targets with astonishing precision," reports suggest the latest strike caused civilian casualties. A local resident told AFP four civilians were affected, though US officials stated "we don't assess there to be any civilian or bystander casualties." Officials acknowledged they were "looking into" civilian casualty reports.

**Transparency Questions**

This marks the third consecutive Pentagon-acknowledged drone strike in Somalia—unusual transparency compared to Yemen operations, where the US has never publicly confirmed specific strikes.
