title: Parents of British man killed by US drone blame UK government
date: March 15, 2013
authors: Chris Woods
content:

Mohamed Sakr, a British-born man, was killed in a US drone strike in Somalia in February 2012. His parents, Gamal and Eman Sakr—Egyptian-born residents of Britain for 35 years—have publicly blamed the UK government for their son's death, arguing that stripping him of citizenship made him vulnerable to the attack.

**Background on Mohamed Sakr:**
- Born in London in 1985
- Initially ran a successful car valeting business in his early twenties
- Traveled to Saudi Arabia and Egypt in summer 2007, which prompted three-hour questioning by UK immigration officials upon return
- Left the UK in October 2009 without informing his family, eventually reaching Somalia

**Government Action:**
In September 2010, the Home Office notified Mohamed that his British citizenship would be revoked on grounds of alleged "involvement in terrorism-related activity." The parents state: "He was brought up here, all his life is here," emphasizing Mohamed had no other passport.

**Family Response:**
The Sakrs renounced their own dual Egyptian nationality to protect their other children from similar deprivation. His father expressed deep pain: "I'll never stop blaming the British government for what they did to my son."

**Contributing Factors:**
- Mohamed's friendship with Bilal al-Berjawi, a childhood neighbor who was similarly stripped of citizenship
- Both men were detained in Kenya in 2009 on suspicion of terrorism-related activities
- Police raids on the family home followed

The article highlights broader concerns about citizenship revocation powers and their consequences.
