title: Study Shows Up to 6 British Extremists Killed in Somalia
date: February 22, 2012
authors: Chris Woods, Emma Slater, Alice Ross, David Pegg
content:

The Bureau of Investigative Journalism released research revealing that up to six UK citizens have died in military strikes conducted by the US and allied forces operating in Somalia. The study was published as world leaders convened in London to discuss the nation's future.

**Key Findings**

The research indicates a significant disparity between British and American military involvement. While the UK has primarily focused on anti-piracy operations, the United States has executed approximately 20 strikes targeting suspected terrorists over five years, resulting in 46 to 162 reported deaths, mostly alleged militants.

**UK Military Operations**

Britain's role has centered on countering piracy through naval operations under Coalition Task Force 150, including board-and-search missions. However, since 2011, UK ground forces have expanded their presence. Notable operations include:

- **June 2011:** Special Boat Squadron completed an eight-week surveillance program mapping pirate ports
- **July 2011:** Royal Marines landed at Berbera in armored vehicles to coordinate with tribal leaders
- **January 2012:** Royal Navy captured 13 Somali pirates

**British Casualties Among Militants**

The study documents British casualties among extremist fighters:

- **January 2007:** Four British militants killed in a US airstrike; SAS troops reportedly collected DNA samples
- **June 2007:** At least one British militant killed in US naval bombardment
- **January 21, 2012:** Bilal al-Barjawi, a British-Lebanese militant, died in a US drone strike

**Strategic Concerns**

Foreign Secretary William Hague signaled increased British involvement, stating the nation must "make it harder for terrorists to operate in and out of Somalia." The Guardian reported contingency plans for potential airstrikes against terrorist targets.

Dr. Micah Zenko of the Council on Foreign Relations noted that "US emphasis is now on disrupting active plots in Somalia against the US homeland," suggesting convergent counter-terrorism interests.
