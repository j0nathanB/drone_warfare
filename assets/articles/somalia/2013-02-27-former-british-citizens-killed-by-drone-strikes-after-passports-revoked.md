title: Former British citizens killed by drone strikes after passports revoked
date: February 27, 2013
authors: Chris Woods, Alice Ross
content:

The Bureau of Investigative Journalism investigation reveals that since 2010, Home Secretary Theresa May revoked passports for 16 individuals with suspected militant connections. At least five were born in Britain, with one resident for nearly 50 years.

**Key Findings:**

The investigation identified cases of British-born or naturalized citizens stripped of citizenship while abroad. Bilal al-Berjawi and Mohamed Sakr, both British nationals, were later killed in US drone strikes after losing their passports—al-Berjawi in 2011 and Sakr in February 2012.

**Critical Concerns:**

Legal experts warned the practice enables government officials to "wash their hands" of citizens facing torture or illegal detention abroad. Ian Macdonald QC stated: "The British government can completely wash their hands if security services give information to Americans who use drones."

The deprivation power—introduced post-9/11—requires only that removal be deemed "conducive to the public good." Appeals occur through semi-secret courts where defendants cannot access full evidence against them.

**Family Impact:**

One case involved a man born in Newcastle in 1963 who, along with three London-born sons, lost citizenship while in Pakistan, effectively preventing family reunification despite the mother's medical needs.

Human rights lawyer Gareth Peirce characterized the situation as resembling "medieval exile, just as cruel and just as arbitrary."
