title: Meet the amputees fighting America's longest war
date: May 22, 2017
authors: Abigail Fielding-Smith
content:

This investigative piece examines Afghan police officers and security force members who continue combat operations despite losing limbs to IED explosions and other war injuries.

### Key Subjects Featured:

**Kudai Rahm Shakir** - Police commander who lost both legs above the knee and returned to frontline duty just 15 days after amputation. He operates his Humvee using hand-controlled pedals and sustained additional bullet wounds to his face and body.

**Pir Mohammed Pamir** - Lost one leg in an IED three years prior; works in anti-narcotics but assists in Taliban defense operations, driving checkpoints despite Taliban proximity.

**Sayed Daoud Saddat** - Lost both legs two years ago; continues with Afghan Local Police, stating employment alternatives are nonexistent for disabled individuals.

**Sardar Mohammed** - Senior officer in Nad Ali district, amputated below the knee, fights from his Humvee. Declined desk reassignment, citing security concerns for his unit.

### Central Issues Documented:

The article reveals that despite "$70 billion" spent on Afghan security since 2002, endemic corruption and mismanagement plague the system. Ghost soldiers, supply diversion, and inadequate resources force amputees into active combat roles when administrative positions should be mandatory. Afghan officials acknowledge this contradicts policy but claim insufficient awareness of such cases persisting in provinces like Helmand.
