title: Changes to how the Bureau calculates Afghan drone and air strike data
date: March 8, 2016
authors: Jack Serle
content:

The Bureau of Investigative Journalism announced modifications to its methodology for tracking U.S. air and drone operations in Afghanistan, effective after more than a year of data collection beginning January 1, 2015.

**Key Methodology Changes:**

Previously, the Bureau categorized strikes as either "confirmed" (reported by U.S. sources or multiple non-U.S. sources) or "possible" (single non-U.S. source). The updated system uses a single category: "reported U.S. strike," sourced from named/unnamed U.S. sources or named Afghan sources. Attacks from unnamed Afghan officials or vague attributions were excluded.

**Data Availability:**

The dataset is now downloadable as a spreadsheet from the Bureau's website, containing casualty information from documented attacks.

**Significant Discrepancies:**

In 2015, the Bureau identified 235 reported strikes, with 166 confirmed by U.S. officials, yet the U.S. Air Force reported conducting 411 strike missions. This disparity likely reflects definitional differences—the Air Force counts "close air support sorties with at least one weapon release," which may involve multiple strikes per sortie.

**Impact of Changes:**

The methodology shift increased the strike count by 37 attacks, accounting for 190-240 deaths (up to seven civilians, including four children). Additionally, twelve previously unreported strikes from January-August 2015 were added to the database.
