title: Islamic State in Afghanistan: Is US assault working?
date: July 28, 2017
authors: Jessica Purkiss, Abigail Fielding-Smith
content:

The Bureau of Investigative Journalism examines the effectiveness of US military operations against Islamic State Khorasan (IS-K) in Afghanistan's Nangarhar province.

**Key Points:**

The US has conducted hundreds of airstrikes against IS-K since January 2016, reducing estimated fighter numbers from 3,000 to approximately 1,000. However, the campaign raises significant concerns about civilian casualties and operational clarity.

**Civilian Casualty Concerns:**

Nangarhar experiences higher civilian casualty rates from US strikes than other Afghan provinces. Following a late June strike, IS-K propaganda outlets posted images of deceased children. When questioned, NATO's Resolute Support stated they "could not conclusively prove that civilian casualties resulted from US air strikes" nor could they disprove it.

**Intelligence Challenges:**

Analysts attribute high civilian casualties to limited human intelligence networks in IS-K-controlled areas, forcing reliance on blanket strike approaches. Researcher Borhan Osman notes that militants' families likely inhabit these territories, compounding identification difficulties.

**Operational Costs:**

The intense campaign—including 27 strikes in one July day—has resulted in six of seven US soldier deaths occurring in Nangarhar.

**Effectiveness Questions:**

Despite the military's pledge to eliminate IS-K by 2017, recruitment continues replacing casualties. Osman observes that "there's a constant flow of people" joining the group, suggesting the campaign may not address underlying appeal factors.
