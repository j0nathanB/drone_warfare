title: US air strike killed Afghan police being held in Taliban prison
date: January 4, 2017
authors: Abigail Fielding-Smith, Ruhullah Khapalwak
content:

### Overview
An investigative report documents a US airstrike in Nad Ali district, Helmand province, Afghanistan on August 25, 2016, that killed Afghan security forces being held as prisoners by the Taliban.

### Key Facts
- Local elders estimated 25-38 prisoners were killed in the strike, primarily Afghan army and police members
- A police commander reported seeing six bodies from the incident
- The Afghan Air Force denied responsibility; the US admitted conducting airstrikes but denied killing captives
- The Bureau interviewed three people with direct knowledge of security force members who died

### Individual Accounts
Muhammad Ikhlas lost his brother, brother-in-law, and nephew in the strike. He expressed confusion about accountability: "We're civilian people. We don't know who is doing these sorts of things."

Muhammad Gul's brother Shadi Khan was captured after his post lacked reinforcements. Khan's final call to his brother: "It was his last phone call to us. We never spoke to him again."

### Policy Context
The US shifted rules in June 2016 to allow proactive Taliban attacks, moving from defensive-only strikes to conventional aerial campaigns with limited ground intelligence from drones and Afghan partners.

### Official Response
Afghan officials initially claimed all victims were Taliban members, later suggesting any security forces killed were traitors. The US military stated confidence there were no civilian casualties.
