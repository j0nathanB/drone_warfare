title: Evidence in British court contradicts CIA drone claims
date: 2012-04-24
authors: Chris Woods
content: |
  A High Court case in London has unveiled fresh evidence regarding civilian casualties from a CIA drone strike in Datta Khel, Pakistan, in March 2011. Sworn affidavits presented in court contradicted CIA assertions that the operation caused no civilian deaths.

  ### Key Details

  **The Incident:** The March 17, 2011 strike killed between 42-53 people, predominantly civilians. The Bureau identified 24 victims by name, while Associated Press reported 42 confirmed civilian deaths.

  **Witness Accounts:** Tribal elder Ahmed Jan, who survived the attack, described the traumatic aftermath: "I was thrown about 24 feet from where I was sitting. When I awoke I saw many individuals who were dead or injured."

  **Official Denials vs. Evidence:** A U.S. official had claimed the casualties were "heavily armed men" connected to al-Qaeda. However, multiple affidavits described victims as community leaders and peacekeepers—including a local policeman and former British Telecom employee—with no militant connections.

  ### Legal Challenge

  The case, brought by legal charity Reprieve and Pakistani advocates, sought judicial review of Britain's alleged provision of surveillance data to U.S. drone operations through GCHQ.

  **Victim Impact:** Families described ongoing consequences, including orphaned children and bodies requiring burial in pieces.
