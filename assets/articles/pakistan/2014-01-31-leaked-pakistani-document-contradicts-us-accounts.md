title: Leaked Pakistani Document Contradicts US Accounts of Drone Strikes
date: 2014-01-31
authors: Alice Ross
content: |
  A confidential Pakistani government document containing records of over 300 drone strikes between 2006 and September 2013 reveals significant discrepancies with US public statements about CIA operations in Pakistan.

  ## Key Findings

  **Document Source and Content:**
  The document was compiled by local officials working with the FATA Secretariat (tribal administration) and includes dates, times, locations, and casualty estimates for strikes. It represents "the fullest official record of the covert campaign yet to emerge."

  **Abu Yahya al Libi Strike (June 2012):**
  US officials claimed video evidence showed a missile killing "just one person." However, independent investigations by Amnesty International and the Bureau found 10-16 total deaths, including six civilians killed in rescue operations. The Pakistani document records 10 deaths, contradicting the US narrative.

  **Dattakhel Jirga Strike (March 2011):**
  US officials dismissed Pakistani claims that tribal elders gathered for traditional mediation, with one stating "these people weren't gathering for a bake sale. They were terrorists." The Pakistani document privately acknowledged "it is feared that all the killed were local tribesmen," supporting Pakistan's public position.

  **Khushali Shop Strike (December 2010):**
  US officials denied a shop was deliberately targeted. The Pakistani document explicitly notes: "US Drone carried out missile strike at a shop in village Khushali Tori Khel."

  ## Broader Context

  The document stops regularly recording civilian casualties after January 2009, though ambiguous language occasionally suggests non-combatants among casualties. Chris Woods, former Bureau drones project director, emphasized that "this secret FATA document...indicates that a shop was indeed hit that day."

  A US official told the Bureau that while "US counter-terrorism operations are precise, lawful, and effective," critics argue the government relies on selective information leaks rather than routine transparency.
