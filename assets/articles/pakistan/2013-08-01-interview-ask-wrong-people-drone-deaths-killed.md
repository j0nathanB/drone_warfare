title: Interview: 'Ask the wrong people about drone deaths and you can be killed'
date: 2013-08-01
authors: Chris Woods
content: |
  This article features an interview with Pakistani journalist Mushtaq Yusufzai, who reports on CIA drone strikes in Waziristan. The piece explores the challenges and dangers of investigative journalism in Pakistan's tribal regions, where access is restricted and sources are difficult to verify.

  ## Key Points

  **The Challenge of Access**

  While Western journalists face restrictions from both Pakistani authorities and militant groups, local reporters can navigate these areas more safely. The Bureau partnered with skilled local journalists to investigate CIA strikes, particularly examining alleged attacks on rescue workers in 2012.

  **Yusufzai's Background**

  Yusufzai benefited from his uncle's reputation as a BBC correspondent, gaining trust among local communities, government officials, and Taliban sources. He built extensive contacts over years of reporting in tribal regions following 9/11.

  **Verification Difficulties**

  The journalist explained that determining casualty status proves extremely difficult. He noted that "most of the time, those who are killed belong to some other places and people don't know their names," making identification challenging without Taliban cooperation—which is rarely forthcoming.

  **Safety Concerns**

  Pakistan ranks among the world's most dangerous countries for journalists, with 44 reporters killed since 2005. Yusufzai acknowledges that aggressive questioning about drone deaths can trigger suspicion and danger from both militants and intelligence agencies.

  **Reporting Standards**

  Yusufzai emphasized the importance of professional independence: "If you're professional and have no hidden agenda," sources will share sensitive information and trust your reporting.
