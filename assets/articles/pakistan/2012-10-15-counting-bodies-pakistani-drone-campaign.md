title: Counting the bodies in the Pakistani drone campaign
date: 2012-10-15
authors: Alice Ross
content: |
  The Bureau of Investigative Journalism published findings showing significant discrepancies in how organizations track civilian casualties from US drone strikes in Pakistan.

  A Columbia University Law School Human Rights Clinic study examined three monitoring organizations tracking drone strikes:

  **Key Findings:**

  The study revealed stark differences in casualty reporting for 2011. Between 72-155 civilians were credibly reported killed according to available media sources. However:

  - New America Foundation reported only 3-9 civilian deaths (a 2,300% underestimate)
  - Long War Journal counted 30 civilians killed
  - The Bureau estimated 68 civilian deaths minimum, deemed most accurate

  The report noted: "The Bureau's data 'appears to have a more methodologically sound count of civilian casualties'" due to employing field researchers and using multiple sources.

  **Core Argument:**

  The study emphasized that relying on media reports creates "estimates, not actual body counts" and called for the US government to release official casualty figures. The report warned against media outlets exclusively citing undercounting organizations, as this risks distorting public understanding of the campaign's true humanitarian impact.

  Chris Woods from the Bureau stressed the urgency for official US transparency regarding casualties in Pakistan and elsewhere.
