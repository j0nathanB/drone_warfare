title: First Month Without a US Drone Strike in Pakistan for Over Two Years
date: 2014-02-03
authors: Jack Serle
content: |
  January 2014 marked a significant milestone as Pakistan experienced its first calendar month without a reported US drone strike in over two years. The previous strike occurred on December 25, 2013, resulting in at least three deaths—notably, the only Christmas Day strike recorded during a decade of drone operations in the country.

  The 40-day pause represents an unusual break in the campaign. Throughout 2013, despite reduced strike frequency, operations averaged roughly one strike every two weeks. The longest prior gap in 2013 lasted 42 days, ending with the death of TTP deputy leader Wali Ur Rehman.

  **Historical Context of Pauses:**

  Previous cessations in drone activity have generally coincided with diplomatic tensions. The last calendar month without strikes occurred in December 2011 during a particularly strained period in US-Pakistan relations, following the Raymond Davis incident, the Osama bin Laden raid, and a mistaken airstrike killing 24 Pakistani border guards.

  **Possible Explanations:**

  Analysts suggest multiple factors could explain the current pause. Reza Jan from the American Enterprise Institute noted the CIA may be exercising greater selectivity, "restricting their target types to more senior fellows" rather than targeting militants regardless of rank.

  Alternatively, Pakistan may have requested the pause, potentially to pursue renewed peace negotiations with the TTP following the controversial death of leader Hakimullah Mehsud in a previous strike.
