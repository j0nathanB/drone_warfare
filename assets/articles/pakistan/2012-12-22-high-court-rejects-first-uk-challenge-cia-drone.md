title: High Court Rejects First UK Challenge to CIA's Drone Campaign
date: 2012-12-22
authors: Alice Ross
content: |
  A London court ruled against judicial review of UK intelligence-sharing practices related to CIA drone strikes, citing concerns about damaging US relations.

  ## Key Details

  The case was brought by Noor Khan, a Pakistani national whose father died in a March 2011 drone strike that killed over 40 people, mostly civilians gathered to resolve a mining dispute.

  Khan's legal team, supported by Reprieve and Leigh Day & Co, argued that GCHQ agents sharing intelligence with US security services—knowing it could enable drone killings—might be committing crimes including accessory to murder.

  ## Court Decision

  Lord Justice Moses and Justice Simon rejected the judicial review application, stating that examining the case would "imperil relations" between states under established legal precedent preventing courts from judging "sovereign acts of a foreign state."

  The judges determined that evaluating whether GCHQ could claim combatant immunity would inevitably place "the United States' conduct in North Waziristan" on trial, making the case unsuitable for judicial examination.

  ## Response

  Khan announced plans to appeal. Rosa Curling of Leigh Day noted the decision "raises very serious questions and issues about the UK's involvement in the CIA drone attacks in Pakistan." Reprieve's Kat Craig stated the ruling demonstrated "a disturbing desire to put itself above the law."
