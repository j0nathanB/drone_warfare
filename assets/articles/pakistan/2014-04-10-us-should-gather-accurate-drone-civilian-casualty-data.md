title: US Should Gather Accurate Drone Civilian Casualty Data, Says Military Analyst
date: 2014-04-10
authors: Patrick Galey
content: |
  A leading U.S. military analyst has urged the government to compile reliable figures on civilian deaths from drone strikes and submit them to Congressional review. Dr. Larry Lewis from the Center for Naval Analyses published a report examining drone casualties in Pakistan, arguing that accurate data collection is essential for national security.

  ## Key Points

  **Main Argument:**
  Lewis contends that "Congress plays a role in shaping and validating US policy through its oversight activities" and that "civilian harm can derail a campaign or undermine US objectives if not handled effectively."

  **Data Concerns:**
  The U.S. has never publicly released its own casualty figures. According to the Bureau's estimates, since 2009 at least 2,183 people have died in drone strikes across Pakistan, Yemen, and Somalia, including approximately 279 civilians.

  **Root Causes Identified:**
  Lewis identifies several factors contributing to civilian casualties:
  - Difficulty assessing damage in remote areas relying on air surveillance
  - Mistaking civilians for combatants based on proximity to targets
  - Logistical challenges fighting irregular enemies

  **Progress Noted:**
  Lewis acknowledged improvements, noting 2013 had "no confirmed civilian deaths" in Pakistan—the first such year since operations began in 2004, though strikes since 2011 still caused civilian casualties roughly 8% of the time.

  **Policy Support:**
  A bipartisan Congressional bill introduced the same week would require President Obama to disclose casualty numbers to Congress, though legislative analysts gave it minimal passage odds.
