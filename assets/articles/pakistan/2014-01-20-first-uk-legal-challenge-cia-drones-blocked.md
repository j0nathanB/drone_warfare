title: First UK Legal Challenge to CIA Drones Blocked by Court of Appeal
date: 2014-01-20
authors: Jack Serle
content: |
  The UK Court of Appeal halted an unprecedented legal case seeking to determine whether British officials were complicit in CIA drone strikes in Pakistan. The case was brought by Noor Khan, whose father was killed in a drone strike on March 17, 2011, along with at least 18 other civilians.

  ## Key Details

  Khan spent two years attempting to compel English courts to examine whether GCHQ shared intelligence about targets with the CIA, potentially making British intelligence officials complicit in murder or war crimes. The British government has consistently refused to confirm or deny such intelligence sharing.

  During the appeal, Khan's legal team presented a hypothetical argument: if a British operative had conducted the drone strikes, would UK officials sharing enabling intelligence constitute a crime?

  ## Court Decision

  The Master of the Rolls acknowledged the argument was "persuasive" but ruled that examining it would require judging a foreign state's actions. The court found "no exceptional circumstances" warranting such review.

  However, the Master of the Rolls noted uncertainty about whether "combatant immunity" would protect a UK national tried for murder via drone strike.

  ## Response

  Khan expressed disappointment: "I used to think that Britain stood for justice, but now it seems as though the Government has put itself above the law."

  His legal representatives argued the courts must retain jurisdiction over government actions regardless of foreign partnerships.
