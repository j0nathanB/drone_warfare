title: Attacking the messenger: how the CIA tried to undermine drone study
date: 2011-08-12
authors: Chris Woods
content: |
  The Bureau of Investigative Journalism faced a coordinated attack from the CIA and unnamed U.S. officials attempting to discredit its comprehensive study of drone strikes in Pakistan.

  ## Main Allegations Against TBIJ

  **Attack on Sources:**
  U.S. counter-terrorism officials attempted to link the Bureau's research to unsubstantiated claims that one of its sources—Pakistani lawyer Mirza Shahzad Akbar—was an ISI (Pakistani intelligence) agent. This characterization aimed to undermine the credibility of the entire investigation.

  **Direct Challenge to Data:**
  CIA officials directly contested the Bureau's findings, with one spokesperson asserting: "We see the battlefield in real time; the Bureau of Investigative Journalism doesn't."

  ## Key Dispute: May 6, 2011 Strike

  The CIA particularly objected to the Bureau's reporting of a North Waziristan drone strike, dismissing claims about a restaurant being hit as "ludicrous." The Bureau documented the incident using 16 credible sources including the New York Times, BBC, and Reuters, identifying casualties including named civilians allegedly killed at the restaurant location.

  ## Response

  Editor Iain Overton suggested the CIA's internal reporting mechanisms had significant gaps, questioning why the agency didn't investigate discrepancies rather than attacking the Bureau's transparent methodology.
