title: Most US Drone Strikes in Pakistan Attack Houses
date: 2014-05-23
authors: Alice Ross, Jack Serle
content: |
  The Bureau of Investigative Journalism, in collaboration with Forensic Architecture and Situ Research, released investigative findings revealing that domestic buildings represent the most frequent targets of CIA drone operations in Pakistan's tribal regions over a decade-long campaign.

  ## Key Findings

  **Target Statistics:**
  - Over 61% of all Pakistani drone strikes targeted domestic buildings, destroying at least 132 houses across more than 380 strikes
  - Approximately 222 civilians were killed in attacks on such structures
  - At least 8 strikes hit mosques and madrassas, proving deadliest with over 17 deaths per attack on average

  **Timing Patterns:**
  Nighttime attacks proved particularly lethal. Researchers discovered that "houses are twice as likely to be attacked at night compared with in the afternoon," with evening strikes being especially deadly when families gathered together.

  **Civilian Impact:**
  Historically, strikes on houses averaged nearly one civilian casualty per attack. However, recent reporting shows "no confirmed reports of civilian casualties in any attacks" over the past 18 months, though strike frequency on houses increased.

  **Comparison with Afghanistan:**
  Notably, since 2008, Afghanistan implemented restrictions on building strikes "in all but the most urgent situations" to protect civilians—a protective measure absent in Pakistan operations.

  **Vehicle Strikes:**
  At least 25% of attacks targeted vehicles, with significantly lower civilian casualty rates and zero confirmed civilian deaths in nighttime vehicle strikes.

  ## Context and Analysis

  Research coordinator Susan Schuppli noted that "women and children's relative seclusion within private space makes them particularly vulnerable to becoming an unknown casualty."

  The investigation included interviews with survivors and analysis of witness testimonies, contemporaneous media reports, and field investigations. An interactive map titled "Where the Drones Strike" visualized strike locations and target types.

  The research highlighted tactical adaptations over the campaign's duration, including modifications to missile explosive yields and introduction of specialized Hellfire variants for different target types.
