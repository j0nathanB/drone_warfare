title: GCHQ Intel Sharing for Drone Strikes May Be 'Accessory to Murder'
date: 2012-10-25
authors: Alice Ross
content: |
  This article covers a landmark judicial review case brought by Pakistani tribesman Noor Khan, whose father was killed in a drone strike. The case examines whether UK intelligence officers at GCHQ may be complicit in potential war crimes by sharing location data with the CIA.

  ## Key Points

  **The Case:**
  Khan's legal team, supported by human rights organization Reprieve, argued that sharing intelligence about militant locations could constitute accessory to murder if those strikes prove unlawful. The case questions whether Pakistan's tribal areas constitute an active war zone, which would affect the legal status of killings there.

  **Government Defense:**
  UK government lawyers contended that a judicial review would damage national security and UK-US relations. One barrister noted: "It would be amazing if the American government was sanguine about an English court saying it's guilty of murder."

  They also argued that since killing alleged militants isn't illegal in the US, British officers cannot be accessories to murder.

  **Legal Complexity:**
  The case hinges on whether drone strikes in Pakistan violate international humanitarian law or constitute unlawful killings, and whether intelligence officers can be held legally responsible for sharing information used in such operations.

  The judges were expected to rule within weeks of the October 23-24 hearing.
