title: Get the Data: The return of double-tap drone strikes
date: 2013-08-01
authors: Chris Woods, Mushtaq Yusufzai
content: |
  The Bureau of Investigative Journalism investigated seven CIA drone strikes in Pakistan's FATA region between May 24 and July 23, 2012. The investigation examined allegations that the agency deliberately targeted rescuers and funeral attendees—tactics known as "double-tap" strikes.

  ## Key Findings

  **May 24, 2012 (Hasukhel):** A makeshift mosque was struck around 4:45 AM, killing four people. Ten to twenty minutes later, "a group of six or seven people including men from the local Dawar tribe arrived to rescue the injured." A second drone attack killed six rescuers immediately, with "around 12 others seriously injured."

  **May 28, 2012:** The investigation found two linked strikes pursued a moving vehicle rather than a stationary target, killing seven alleged militants. Four civilians were injured nearby.

  **June 3, 2012:** Commanders attended funeral prayers for a relative killed in a prior strike. The investigation found no deaths, contrary to initial reports.

  **June 4, 2012:** Multiple strikes killed al Qaeda's number two, Yahya al-Libi. The Bureau's research indicated "as many as 16 people, all alleged militants, died in several linked strikes," including those engaged in rescue operations.

  **June 14, 2012:** Contrary to claims of a destroyed building and multiple casualties, findings showed "only one person, believed to be an Arab national, was killed."

  **July 6, 2012:** After initial strikes on a group eating dinner, rescue workers were attacked approximately 30 minutes later, killing 12 additional people.

  **July 23, 2012:** Following an initial strike killing five alleged militants, rescuers were targeted, resulting in seven deaths and eight injuries.

  ## Significance

  The Bureau's 2012 investigation had documented similar targeting patterns between 2009-2011, which UN rapporteurs characterized as potential war crimes. This 2013 investigation suggested the practice had resumed after a July 2011 hiatus.
