title: Drone strikes continue but the dead die unnamed
date: 2014-12-12
authors: Jack Serle
content: |
  The Bureau of Investigative Journalism's "Naming the Dead" project documented a troubling trend in 2014: only 30 of 104 people reported killed in Pakistani drone strikes were identified by name—a significant decline from 2013 when nearly all victims were named.

  **Key Findings:**

  The article attributes this identification gap to restricted access in Pakistan's Federally Administered Tribal Areas (FATA). A military operation beginning June 15, 2014 displaced over one million people and made independent journalism nearly impossible.

  "You can't really do much independent reporting, even now," according to Pakistani journalist Rahimullah Yousufzai, who noted that international journalists require government permission and Pakistani journalists face severe safety risks.

  Information sources have narrowed considerably. Rather than witnesses and tribal leaders providing details, most 2014 victim information came from al Qaeda or unnamed Pakistani officials. The military's media wing (ISPR) became the primary news source, frequently reporting deaths of unnamed Central Asians.

  **Historical Context:**

  The 2014 figures represent the lowest identification rate since drone strikes began in 2004, though 2010 saw only 114 of 751 victims named during another intensive military operation period.
