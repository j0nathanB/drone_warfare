title: First UK Legal Challenge to CIA Drones Reaches Court of Appeal
date: 2013-12-04
authors: Jack Serle
content: |
  A landmark judicial review case challenging potential British complicity in CIA drone operations reached the UK Court of Appeal this week. Pakistani tribesman Noor Khan brought the case after his father was killed in a March 2011 drone strike in Pakistan that resulted in over 40 civilian deaths.

  ## Key Issues

  Khan's legal team sought to establish whether GCHQ officials share intelligence with the CIA regarding drone targets in Pakistan. The case questions whether such intelligence-sharing could make British intelligence operatives accessories to murder or war crimes.

  The lawsuit was filed against Foreign Secretary William Hague, who oversees GCHQ. The British government has maintained a consistent policy of neither confirming nor denying intelligence-sharing arrangements with American agencies.

  ## Legal Arguments

  Khan's barrister Martin Chamberlain QC argued that the case did not constitute an attack on US conduct, stating: "The criminality of agents of the United States is a matter for their law or Pakistan's."

  The government's legal representatives countered that proceeding with the case would force courts to examine American drone strike legality, potentially damaging UK-US relations.

  Chamberlain noted that the government's filing of a Public Interest Immunity certificate suggested officials possessed information they wished to withhold from public scrutiny.
